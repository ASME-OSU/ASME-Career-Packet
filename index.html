Highest-Impact Moves First

For when the event is soon and you need to prioritize. These three moves alone will set you apart.

03 — Types of Experience

Internship vs. Co-op vs. Externship

Understanding the difference helps you target the right opportunity for your goals and schedule.

Experience🎓 Internship🔄 Co-op👁 Externship
DurationA few months — usually summer or one semesterLong-term; alternates full semesters of work with full semesters of schoolA few days to a few weeks — very short
PayUsually paidAlmost always paid — often higher than internshipsUsually unpaid
Work typeReal engineering work on a team or projectDeep, structured — treated more like a junior engineerShadowing and observation — limited hands-on
Best forReal-world experience and resume evidence without long-term commitmentStudents wanting deep industry immersion and a strong path to a return offerExploring a career path early before committing to an internship
Full-time pathSometimes leads to a return offer — not guaranteedStrong conversion path — many co-ops become full-time hiresGood for early connections; rarely leads directly to a hire
Graduation impactMinimal — usually summer onlyMay delay graduation; requires schedule flexibilityMinimal — can fit around a semester

Which is right for you? Externship = explore quickly. Internship = build evidence without delaying graduation. Co-op = deepest experience and strongest return-offer path, if you can handle the schedule trade-off. Your career journey is unique — choose what aligns with your goals, not just what sounds most impressive.

04 — Your Brand

Building Your Professional Brand

Your brand is the thread that connects your resume, elevator pitch, LinkedIn, and interview answers. Get clear on it first — everything else becomes easier.

What is your brand?

It's what you want people to think of when they think of you professionally.

What sets you apart?

The combination of your skills, experiences, and personality that makes you a different kind of candidate.

How do you want to be perceived?

Resourceful? Detail-oriented? Systems thinker? Strong communicator? Pick 3 words and build toward them.

What do you offer?

What can you do for a team that another intern can't? Be specific — broad claims don't make brands.

What are your goals?

Manufacturing, design, aerospace, robotics, controls, energy — know your direction and let it shape everything you say.

Why your brand matters

Think of companies you recognize instantly — their brand is cohesive across every touchpoint. Your brand should work the same way: your resume, LinkedIn, elevator pitch, and interview answers should all feel like they come from the same person with the same direction.

The worst thing you can do in an interview is give a generic answer that any college engineer could provide. Strong candidates weave 3 defining characteristics through everything they say.

Practical exercise: Write down three words that describe the engineer you want to be known as. Now ask yourself: does your resume reflect those words? Does your LinkedIn? If you walked into a career fair tomorrow and a recruiter asked "tell me about yourself" — do those words come through? If not, that's where to start.
05 — Resume Builder

Resume by Year

Your resume evolves as your evidence grows. The goal: make your strongest proof impossible to miss.

First Year

Show potential and momentum.

Your first resume can include strong high school experience — frame it as engineering evidence, not nostalgia.

Lead with

Education, graduation year, GPA if strong, ASME, relevant coursework, early project teams.

Include

Robotics, CAD, machining, coding, shop work, part-time jobs you can connect to engineering skills.

Avoid

Long high school lists, vague awards, and skills you can't explain in a 2-minute interview.

Goal

Show curiosity, reliability, and willingness to learn fast. Potential beats a thin résumé.

Sophomore

Show direction and early specialization.

  • Move college projects above high school when they're stronger — this usually happens mid-sophomore year.
  • Add class projects that had design constraints, calculations, prototypes, or test data.
  • Translate non-engineering jobs into reliability, training, safety, or operations skills.
  • Start tailoring toward a track: design, manufacturing, aerospace, robotics, controls.
Junior

Show readiness for real engineering work.

The top half of the page should answer: "Can this person contribute this summer?" Lead with internships, research, competitions, and technical leadership.

  • Use metrics where possible: weight reduced, cycle time improved, prototypes built, data sets analyzed, budget managed.
  • Replace filler with role-relevant technical experience — every line should earn its place.
  • Be concrete: name constraints, tools, your specific decisions, what you tested, and what happened.
  • Build multiple tailored versions for different role types if you're applying broadly.
Senior

Show ownership, judgment, and outcomes.

  • Lead with full-time-relevant experience: internships, capstone, research, and technical leadership roles.
  • Show engineering judgment: tradeoffs you considered, risks you identified, validation you ran, documentation you produced.
  • Add publications, patents, conference presentations, or professional certifications if applicable.
  • Prepare a matching portfolio or project page for design-heavy or research roles.

Resume format non-negotiables: One page. Common, readable font — no smaller than 11pt. Bold position titles. Clear sections separated with lines. No walls of text. No photos of yourself. No MS Word templates (build from scratch or use a clean Overleaf/LaTeX template). Save and submit as PDF named Firstname_Lastname_Resume.pdf.

ATS — Applicant Tracking Systems: Most large companies run resumes through software before a human sees them. ATS systems scan for keywords from the job description. Use the exact terminology from the posting (e.g. "CAD" vs "SolidWorks"), avoid tables and text boxes (they confuse parsers), and don't put critical info in headers/footers. Tailor your skills section to match each job posting's language — it takes 5 minutes and meaningfully improves your chances of getting through.

Writing Strong Bullets

Action verb + technical method/tool + scope + quantified outcome.

  • Designed a 3D-printed bracket in SolidWorks to mount a sensor package, reducing assembly time by 30%.
  • Analyzed heat transfer data in MATLAB across 40 trials to identify insulation changes that improved thermal retention.
  • Led a 5-person subteam through prototype testing, documenting failure modes and presenting recommendations to project leads.
  • Modeled gearbox housing using tolerance constraints for 6 machined interfaces; validated fit with a physical check fixture.

06 — LinkedIn

LinkedIn Done Right

LinkedIn is social media for your career — it's your resume with more room, and a pipeline to maintain your network. People actually look at it.

What to include

  • Professional headshot — clear, well-lit, you're the focus.
  • Headline beyond "Student" — e.g., ME Student @ OSU | Manufacturing & Design | ASME VP | FAA Part 107
  • 2–3 sentence summary covering your interests, experiences, and what you're looking for — not a bulleted list.
  • All resume experience plus anything that didn't fit — research, projects, certifications, awards.
  • 2–4 project entries with images, tools used, and short descriptions.
  • Skills section — add specific technical tools, not just "leadership" and "teamwork."

Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Post about internships, project milestones, and wins — be excited and professional.
  • Connect with people you actually meet — career fairs, speakers, alumni, classmates.
  • DM companies and recruiters whose work interests you — a short, specific message beats no message.
  • Keep it current — update after every significant experience.

✗ Don't

  • Fabricate or misrepresent anything — interviewers will ask about it.
  • Post extremely often or in very long messages — brief and specific wins.
  • Spam people with identical copy-paste outreach — it's obvious and off-putting.
  • Accept every single connection request — 500+ meaningless connections won't help you.
Pro tip: Don't post about a job until you're certain you're taking it. Accepting then reneging after a public post is a bad look that can follow you. Post-internship updates talking about what you worked on are some of the most valuable content you can share.
07 — Elevator Pitch

Your 30-Second Introduction

Within the time you enter an elevator to when you leave — under 30 seconds — you should be able to introduce yourself compellingly. Practice until it feels natural, not rehearsed.

The formula

  • Name & year — "Hi, I'm [Name], a [year] mechanical engineering student at Ohio State."
  • One key experience — "This past summer I interned at [company], where I [specific thing you did]."
  • Involvement — "I'm also involved in [ASME / Buckeye Vertical / project team], where I [role]."
  • Target — "I'm looking for [internship / co-op / full-time] roles in [area]."
  • Hook — "I saw your team does [specific thing] — I'd love to hear more about what you're working on."

Rules

30–60 seconds max. Don't just talk at them — ask a question, start a conversation. Roll with it if they take it somewhere interesting. Practice & experiment. You never know who you'll meet — always be ready.

08 — Cover Letter

Cover Letters

Only write one for roles you genuinely want. A thoughtful, specific letter can separate you — a generic one hurts you.

Cover Letter Structure

One page. Formal letter format. Specific — never generic.

1

Opening paragraph

State the role, show genuine interest in this specific company, and preview your strongest relevant experience. Don't start with "I am writing to apply for…" — it's the most common opener and signals zero effort.

2

Body paragraph(s)

Explain why this company specifically, connect your qualifications to their job description, and tell the story behind your resume bullets instead of just listing them again.

3

Closing paragraph

Thank them, provide contact info, and express genuine enthusiasm for the opportunity to discuss further. Close with "Sincerely," — not "Best regards" if you want to sound less generic.

When to write one

  • Roles you genuinely want — a thoughtful letter signals real interest.
  • Small or mid-size companies where hiring decisions are more personal.
  • When the application explicitly asks for one.
  • When you have something important to contextualize: non-traditional background, gap, career shift.

What to avoid

  • Don't copy your resume — explain the story behind it.
  • Don't start every sentence with "I."
  • Don't use phrases like "I am a hard worker" or "I'm a team player."
  • Don't send the same letter to every company — recruiters can tell.
  • Don't exceed one page.
09 — Project Portfolio

Building a Project Portfolio

Increasingly expected for design, manufacturing, and research roles. A portfolio is proof that a resume can only describe.

What to include

  • Images first — CAD models, prototype photos, test setups, code outputs, FEA plots — visuals that can't go on a resume are the whole point.
  • Project descriptions — What was the problem? What did you do? What were the results? Concise but specific.
  • Your role — Be clear about what you personally did, especially in team projects. Don't take credit for things you didn't do — interviewers can tell.
  • Order by relevance to the role you're applying for, not by date.
  • Tailor per company — Move aerospace projects first for aerospace roles, manufacturing first for manufacturing.

Entry formula: What / How / Results

What?

One sentence on the project context and the problem you were solving.

How?

2–3 sentences: your approach, tools used, key design or engineering decisions made.

Results

Quantify where possible: weight saved, test passed, competition placement, efficiency gained.

Format: PDF is safest for email. A personal site or GitHub Pages makes sharing instant. Canva works for polished one-pagers if you don't want to code. Keep it under 10 projects — quality over quantity.
10 — Dress Code

Decoding Dress Codes

When in doubt, dress one level up. It's always easier to remove a blazer than to wish you'd worn one.

Business CasualInfo sessions, networking events
  • Slacks or chinos — khaki, navy, gray, black
  • Collared shirt or blouse, tucked or clean-cut
  • Optional blazer or cardigan
  • Clean, closed-toe shoes — loafers, flats, low heels
  • No athletic wear, hoodies, or overly casual jeans
Business ProfessionalCareer fairs, phone & first-round interviews
  • Blazer or suit jacket — matched or coordinated
  • Dress shirt or blouse with collar; ties optional for men
  • Dress slacks or pencil skirt
  • Polished dress shoes or professional heels
  • Minimal jewelry; professional accessories only
Business FormalFinal-round interviews, technical presentations
  • Full matching suit — dark navy, charcoal, or black
  • Dress shirt + tie (men) or tailored blouse/suit (women)
  • Conservative, polished shoes — no exceptions
  • Minimal accessories — nothing distracting
  • Grooming matters: clean and professional throughout

Virtual interviews: Still dress up from the waist up as if you're in person. Check your background — clean wall or bookshelf beats a messy room. Good lighting matters more than you think: face a window or buy a $15 ring light. Log in 5 minutes early. Keep water nearby — taking a sip before answering gives you time to think and formulate your response.

11 — Interview Repository

Behavioral & Technical Interviews

The goal: prepare enough structure that you can be relaxed and specific. Specific beats polished every time.

Behavioral interviews — STAR method

Use STAR: Situation → Task → Action → Result.

  • Situation: Why the problem existed. Keep this brief — 2–3 sentences.
  • Task: Your scope. Did you identify the problem or were you assigned it?
  • Action: What you did and why. This is the longest part — your reasoning matters as much as your actions.
  • Result: The outcome. Quantify where possible. If it was a failure, what did you learn and what did you change?
Timing: Each response should be under 2 minutes. Speak slower than you think you should. Be excited — enthusiasm is a differentiator, especially for internship candidates where everyone has limited experience.
Technical interviews — reason out loud

State your assumptions out loud. Draw the system if allowed. Name tradeoffs you see. Admit what you'd need to verify. Interviewers care about your reasoning process far more than whether you recall the formula instantly.

If a company is going to give you a technical interview, they will usually tell you in advance. If they don't mention it — it's likely behavioral.

Strong answer structure:

  • Restate the problem and clarify constraints.
  • Name the principles involved before jumping to equations.
  • Explain what information is missing and how you would estimate it.
  • Close with a validation step: test, inspect, simulate, or compare to expected behavior.
Technical presentation interviews

Some companies ask you to present past project work on a slideshow. Cover: the problem, your role, key technical decisions, challenges you hit, and how you resolved them. Practice explaining tradeoffs — not just final answers. Keep slides visual and light on text. Deliver it like a design review for a class.

Simple slide flow:

  • Problem, constraints, and why it mattered.
  • Your role, tools used, and design alternatives considered.
  • Testing, results, failure modes, and what changed.
  • What you would improve with more time or better data.
When your mind goes blank mid-interview

It happens — don't panic. Take your time. It's better to sit for 30–45 seconds and give a good answer than to rush and give a bad one.

Recovery approach:

  • Think through your orgs and experiences — internships, project teams, classes, research.
  • Start with the first one that comes to mind.
  • Think about what you did that week or your main projects.
  • Then brainstorm on your specific actions and results from there.
Prevention: Use AI to practice. Prompt: "Generate behavioral interview questions for this job posting and my resume" — then paste both. Practice answering out loud, not in your head. Get a friend to grill you at least once before a real interview.

Building your story bank: Brainstorm your best moments from every internship, project team, class, and research position. Make a notecard per story — one per experience — and note which types of questions each story could answer. One story can often work for leadership, conflict, failure, and time management questions depending on how you frame it. More recent stories are generally stronger. Don't exaggerate scope or ownership — interviewers can tell, and it's not worth the risk.

Going forward: Keep a running doc during your internships where you record stories as they happen. The best stories often don't have the biggest scope — short, clear examples of your problem-solving and leadership style can be your strongest answers.

Behavioral

Core prompts

  • Tell me about yourself.
  • Walk me through a project you're proud of.
  • Tell me about a time something failed.
  • How do you handle team conflict?
  • Describe a time you had to learn something quickly.
  • Tell me about a time you had too many priorities at once.
  • Tell me about a time you received constructive feedback.
  • Why this company and this specific role?
Technical

Mechanical prompts

  • How would you design a bracket for load and manufacturability?
  • Explain stress, strain, and factor of safety.
  • How would you validate a thermal design?
  • What causes vibration or fatigue failure?
  • How do tolerances affect assembly?
  • How would you choose between two materials?
  • Walk me through how you'd approach a simulation.
Ask Them

Candidate questions

  • What would an intern help solve in the first month?
  • What separates strong interns from average ones?
  • What do you find most interesting about your own work?
  • How do engineers on this team collaborate with manufacturing or quality?
  • What would you want me to strengthen before day one?
  • How is feedback given during the internship?

After the interview: Send a follow-up thank-you email to your interviewer that day or the next morning. Connect with them on LinkedIn. If you haven't heard back in ~2 weeks, send a polite status-check email to the recruiter who set up the interview. See the email templates section for exact wording.

12 — Technical Training

Technical Prep Map

An ASME seminar guide for CAD, FEA, GD&T, manufacturing, and design reviews — the topics that come up most in mechanical engineering interviews.

CAD Training

Build models that communicate intent.

Know sketches, constraints, assemblies, mates, drawings, section views, exploded views, and design tables. Be ready to explain why you modeled something in a certain order — that reasoning is what interviewers are listening for, not speed.

FEA Training

Use simulation as a decision tool.

Know loads, constraints, mesh sensitivity, stress concentration, factor of safety, and the difference between pretty color plots and validated analysis. Always connect simulation results back to your assumptions and explain what you would do differently with more time or resources.

GD&T

Design parts that can actually be made and inspected.

Learn datums, flatness, perpendicularity, position, clearance, and tolerance stack-ups. Understand why over-tolerancing increases cost. In interviews, be ready to explain how your tolerance choices affect the final assembly — not just the drawing.

Manufacturing

Think beyond the model.

Be familiar with machining, 3D printing, sheet metal, injection molding, welding, casting, surface finish, fastening, and design for assembly. Employers love students who consider cost and process from the very start of a design — not after the model is done.

Design Review

Present tradeoffs, not just final answers.

A strong design review covers: requirements, assumptions, concepts considered, the selected design, risks, test plan, and next steps. Practice explaining what you would change given more time, money, or a different constraint. That's what separates strong presenters from ones who only show the happy path.

13 — Career Fair Playbook

Career Fair Strategy

Research, prioritize, start conversations well, and follow up quickly. That's the entire playbook.

1 Week Out

Build your target list.

Pick 8–12 employers. For each: role type, location, one product area, and one reason you're genuinely interested. Download Career Fair Plus to map company locations and plan your route.

2 Days Out

Apply online before you go.

Some companies prefer online applications before the fair. Get yours in — then you can say "I already applied" at the booth, which shows real initiative.

Night Before

Prep your materials.

Print 20–30 resumes (more than you think you need). Have a digital copy with a clean filename ready on your phone to upload on the spot. Write down one question per target company. Lay out your outfit.

Event Day

Approach, pitch, ask — then connect.

Firm handshake, introduce yourself, deliver your pitch, let them talk, ask your question. End by offering to connect on LinkedIn. After each company, write down the recruiter's name, what they said, and one personal detail to reference in your follow-up.

24 Hours After

Follow up while they remember you.

Send a short thank-you email referencing something specific from the conversation. Apply online if you haven't. Connect on LinkedIn.

Be the Question Master

Don't ask…

  • Questions you could Google in 10 seconds
  • Generic questions every other student asks
  • Questions about pay at the booth

Do ask…

  • About the person you're talking to and their experience
  • Specific questions about the role or team
  • What makes strong intern candidates stand out

Application tracker fields

  • Company, role title, location, deadline, application link.
  • Recruiter name, email, LinkedIn — notes from your conversation.
  • Skills mentioned, projects to emphasize for that company, follow-up date.
  • Status: interested → applied → referred → interview → offer.
Network before the fair: If ASME hosts a corporate banquet or company info sessions before the big fair, go. Talk to recruiters there. Showing up to IR or EXPO with an existing connection is a massive advantage — "We talked at the banquet" is a much stronger opener than "I'm a junior in ME."
14 — Info Sessions

Employer Info Sessions

Attending signals genuine interest. It also gives you insider knowledge to use in your cover letter, interview, and follow-up emails.

Before

How to prepare

  • Visit the company website; read about their products, recent news, and competitors.
  • Update your resume and bring printed copies if in person.
  • Prepare 3–4 specific questions — not things easily Googleable.
  • For virtual: test your tech 24 hours before. Clean background, stable connection.
During

What to do

  • Arrive or log in early — don't be the person joining late.
  • Take notes on speaker names, team details, and anything unique that you can reference later.
  • Engage actively — ask your prepared question publicly when given the chance.
  • When prompted to introduce yourself: name, major, year, and one specific interest.
After

Follow up

  • Connect on LinkedIn with any recruiter you interacted with.
  • Send a follow-up email within 24 hours — mention something specific you learned.
  • Apply to open positions and reference your attendance in the cover letter.
  • Engage with the company's LinkedIn posts — stay on their radar between now and the hire.
15 — Networking Toolkit

Networking

Networking is building a group of people who support you professionally — not asking strangers for jobs. It's nothing more complicated than building relationships with people you meet and work with.

The mindset shift: Your network is a two-way street. Senior engineers and hiring managers often want to meet students — they're looking for qualified people to hire and they want to share their experience with the next generation. You're not imposing. Show up, introduce yourself, and be genuinely curious about what they do.

Three steps, that's it: Form connections → Stay in touch → Don't be afraid to ask for favors (the right way).

Coffee Chats

The most underused tool

If you recently connected with someone — in person, via LinkedIn, or through a mutual contact — asking for 30 minutes of their time is one of the best moves you can make.

  • Your goal isn't a referral or a job — it's to learn and build a real relationship.
  • Go in with 5–7 questions about them: their decisions, their lessons, not just their resume.
  • Follow the flow of conversation — don't robotically run through a list.
  • End by asking if there's anyone else you should reach out to.
  • Send a thank-you note referencing something specific they said. Update them a few months later on what you did with their advice.
Staying in Touch

After the first conversation

  • If you met someone briefly — email or LinkedIn message to follow up after the fact. Attach your resume.
  • For longer-term relationships: keep them updated on career updates, project milestones, things you talked about.
  • If they gave you advice, update them on the outcome — this is one of the rarest and most appreciated things a student can do.
  • Follow up every 3–6 months — a short "here's what I've been up to" message keeps you on their radar without being annoying.
Asking for Favors

The right way

It's okay to ask favors — but understand you're asking someone to vouch for you. Be clear, specific, and polite. Only ask if you're genuinely interested. Never ask someone to refer you to a role you're not sure you want — and never accept an offer and then renege. Engineering is a small world.

  • Be excited about what you do — enthusiasm is contagious and memorable.
  • Be concise — they're busy. One clear ask is better than a long buildup.
  • Thank them regardless of outcome.
16 — Internship Success

Making the Most of Your Internship

Getting the internship is step one. The best interns create clarity, document their impact, and leave having built relationships that last past the summer.

Before Day One

Arrive with context.

Review the company's products, tools, locations, and safety expectations. Refresh any core skills that appear in the job posting. Build an acronym dictionary from day one — every company has its own language and it's overwhelming at first. Make a list, then learn it.

First Week

Carry a notebook. Always.

Write down expectations, questions, acronyms you don't know, and names of everyone you meet. Ask clarifying questions early — ask what success looks like, who the key stakeholders are, what your deliverables are, and how often your manager wants updates. Don't let ambiguity become your biggest blocker. Internships are short — get up to speed as fast as possible.

During the Internship

Keep an impact log. Be the person who gets things done.

  • Log your tasks, tools used, decisions made, results, and feedback quotes at least weekly — this becomes your final presentation and your next resume.
  • Be the person people know they can go to. Be resourceful, ask clarifying questions, and be excited to be there.
  • If you have nothing to do — seek projects. No project is below you. People notice who's looking for ways to help.
  • Know your customer: what does your boss actually want, and when? You could make the most polished deliverable in the world — but if it doesn't solve their real problem, it's not worth much.
  • Send a brief update email to your manager every 1–2 weeks if you don't see them often: "Here's what I worked on, here's where I am, here's what's next."
Final Week

Close the loop like a professional.

Send documentation. Thank your mentors — the ones who answered the most questions often don't get thanked enough. Request written or verbal feedback you can act on. Connect with everyone on LinkedIn. Ask what skills would strengthen your case for a return offer, and what you should keep working on. The engineering world is small — the relationships you build in 12 weeks can matter for 10 years.

17 — Evaluating Offers

Getting & Evaluating Offers

Receiving an offer is exciting. Take a breath, evaluate carefully, and respond professionally — whether you're accepting, negotiating, or declining.

What to evaluate

  • Compensation: Hourly rate, overtime policy, sign-on bonuses.
  • Housing / relocation stipend: Is it included? Is it enough for the city's cost of living?
  • Location: Commute, cost of living, quality of life, whether you want to live there for the summer.
  • Work type: Will you be doing real engineering work or support tasks? Ask about past intern projects.
  • Mentorship & structure: Will you have a dedicated mentor and a defined project?
  • Return offer potential: Does this company typically convert interns? What's the conversion rate?
  • Brand and optionality: Does this company name open doors for future roles?
  • Your gut: Did the people you met seem like people you'd actually learn from?

Responding to an offer

  • Ask for time — it's completely standard to ask for 1–2 weeks to consider. Most companies expect this.
  • Negotiating — for internships, base pay is often fixed, but housing stipends, start dates, and location are sometimes flexible. Always be polite and professional — never aggressive.
  • Accepting — confirm in writing immediately. Do not renege after accepting — it damages your reputation permanently and can follow you back to your school's recruiting relationships.
  • Declining — do it graciously and quickly. Thank them sincerely, keep the door open, and be honest about your reasons if they ask. The engineering world is small.
Waiting on another offer? Never accept with the intent to renege. If you're waiting on a higher-priority company, be honest about your timeline and ask for a reasonable extension. Most companies would rather wait a week than lose a candidate they want — but don't push it past two weeks without a very good reason.

18 — No Internship? No Problem.

Alternative Ways to Build Experience

Not landing an internship in one cycle doesn't define your trajectory. Cast a wide net — and in the meantime, here are the paths that keep you competitive.

Undergraduate Research

Reach out to faculty whose work interests you. Even one semester of lab experience becomes a strong resume entry — and it's highly valued for grad school applications. Use the outreach formula in the Grad School section.

Design Competitions

AUVSI SUAS, Formula SAE, Baja, SolarSplash, AIAA DBF. Treat these like internships: document what you did, what tools you used, and what the outcomes were. Competition placements are legitimate engineering evidence.

Personal Projects

Build something. A 3D-printed mechanism, an IoT device, a custom PCB, an autonomous system. Document it with photos, write-ups, and a GitHub or portfolio link. The project doesn't have to be huge — it has to be real and explainable.

Volunteer Engineering

Engineering for nonprofits, Habitat for Humanity, local makerspaces, or community organizations. Real constraints, real deliverables — that's engineering experience by another name, and it shows character.

Certifications

SOLIDWORKS CSWA/CSWP, GD&T certification, Python/MATLAB courses, Excel & Power BI, manufacturing process certificates, FAA Part 107. Specific credentials signal initiative and fill skill gaps recruiters actually care about.

Externships & Shadowing

A week shadowing an engineer at a local manufacturer or energy company counts. Reach out through LinkedIn, alumni networks, or your engineering college's career services. One week can turn into a referral.

Reframe the narrative: If you had a gap semester, took extra credits, dealt with a family situation, or just had a rough recruiting cycle — you can still tell a compelling story. Interviewers care about what you did with the time and what you learned from it. Be honest, be forward-looking, and don't over-explain.

19 — Research & Grad School

Research & Grad School

Not every student is industry-bound immediately. This section helps you explore research, fellowships, and graduate applications.

Faculty outreach formula

  • Read at least one lab page or recent publication abstract before reaching out — don't cold-email without context.
  • Write a short email connecting your interest to their specific research, not just their department.
  • Attach a resume and mention relevant coursework, tools, or projects by name.
  • Ask if they're taking undergraduate researchers or if a 15-minute conversation is possible.
  • Follow up once after 1–2 weeks if you hear nothing back.

Grad school planning timeline

  • Identify research areas before choosing programs — advisor fit matters more than institution ranking for most grad students.
  • Build relationships with recommenders at least one full semester before you need letters. Give them a resume, a list of programs, and a draft statement — make it easy for them.
  • Track deadlines for fellowships (NSF GRFP opens early fall), statements of purpose, transcripts, and any required tests.
  • Your statement of purpose should explain research fit, relevant preparation, and your future direction — not just restate your GPA and activities.
